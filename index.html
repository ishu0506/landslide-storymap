Spatio-Temporal Landslide Susceptibility Mapping

Gilgit District, Pakistan

2017–2024
Using GIS • Remote Sensing • Machine Learning • SHAP

University of Gujrat

Department of Geography — Remote Sensing & GIS

Supervisor: Dr. Saira Munawar, Assistant Professor, Department of Geography, University of Gujrat

Chairperson: Dr. Muhammad Miandad Abbasi, Department of Geography, University of Gujrat

Introduction

Landslides are among the most destructive natural hazards in mountainous terrain, driven by the interaction of steep slopes, active tectonics, heavy rainfall and expanding human activity. Gilgit District sits at the junction of the Karakoram, Hindu Kush and Himalayan mountain systems, where continuing plate collision and rugged relief make slope failure a recurring threat to roads, settlements and livelihoods.

This StoryMap presents a Landslide Susceptibility Mapping (LSM) framework built for Gilgit District covering 2017–2024. Twelve geo-environmental conditioning factors were combined with a verified landslide inventory and modelled using a Random Forest classifier, while SHAP (Explainable AI) was used to interpret which factors drive susceptibility and why — turning a "black box" prediction into a transparent, decision-ready hazard map.

Study Area

Gilgit District lies between 35°40′–36°40′N and 73°40′–74°40′E in Gilgit-Baltistan, northern Pakistan, spanning roughly 4,208 km². Elevation ranges from about 1,213 m in the river valleys to 7,647 m in the surrounding peaks, with slopes frequently exceeding 35°.

The district is drained by the Gilgit and Hunza Rivers and crossed by the Karakoram Highway — a critical CPEC transportation corridor. Active tectonic uplift, fractured rock formations, seasonal rainfall and snowmelt combine to make this one of the most landslide-prone regions of northern Pakistan.

Methodology

A landslide inventory of 104 points — 52 landslide and 52 non-landslide locations (2017–2024) — was built using Google Earth Pro imagery and disaster records, then split 70:30 for model training and testing.

Twelve conditioning factors were extracted from DEM, Sentinel-2 and GPM-IMERG data, checked for multicollinearity using VIF and Tolerance, then used to train a Random Forest classifier. SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) was applied afterward to interpret each factor's contribution before generating the final susceptibility map.

Landslide Conditioning Factors

Twelve topographic, hydrological, environmental and anthropogenic factors were used to train the Random Forest model.

Elevation

Derived from a 30 m DEM; shapes rainfall patterns, vegetation zones and snowmelt behaviour across the district.

Slope

The steepest angle of terrain change — the single most influential factor, directly linked to gravitational shear stress.

Aspect

Direction a slope faces; influences solar exposure, moisture retention and freeze–thaw weathering cycles.

Curvature

Describes concave vs. convex terrain; concave zones trap water and loose soil, raising instability.

Profile Curvature

Curvature along the slope direction; controls runoff velocity and downslope erosion intensity.

TWI

Topographic Wetness Index estimates soil moisture accumulation; higher values mean wetter, less stable ground.

SPI

Stream Power Index measures the erosive power of surface runoff across the terrain.

LULC

Land Use/Land Cover from ESA WorldCover; barren and sparsely vegetated land is far more landslide-prone than forest cover.

NDVI

Vegetation density and root cohesion from Sentinel-2; denser vegetation improves soil stability.

Precipitation

GPM-IMERG rainfall data; increased rainfall raises pore-water pressure, a key landslide trigger.

Distance from Rivers

Proximity to river channels; nearby slopes face continual toe erosion and bank undercutting.

Distance from Roads

Proximity to road networks; cutting, blasting and excavation activities destabilise nearby slopes.

Multicollinearity Check

VIF and Tolerance values for all 12 factors — every factor fell within acceptable limits (VIF < 5, TOL > 0.2), confirming no redundant variables.

The Random Forest model achieved an AUC of 0.94 — excellent discrimination between landslide and non-landslide zones.

Final Landslide Susceptibility Map

All twelve factors were combined through the trained Random Forest model to classify Gilgit District into five susceptibility zones.

84.4% Overall Accuracy
87.0% Precision
81.0% Recall
84.0% F1-Score
0.94 ROC-AUC

High and Very High susceptibility zones cluster along steep valley slopes, river courses and the Karakoram Highway corridor — where SHAP identified Slope, NDVI, Distance from Roads, Distance from Rivers, TWI and Elevation as the strongest drivers.

Model Interpretability

SHAP (Explainable AI) was used to open up the Random Forest "black box" and explain what drove each prediction.

Feature Importance

Random Forest ranks Slope as the single most important factor, followed by NDVI, Distance from Roads, Distance from Rivers, TWI and Elevation.

SHAP Summary Plot

Shows how each factor pushes predictions higher or lower. Steep slopes and low vegetation (NDVI) strongly increase susceptibility, while distance from roads and rivers reduces it.

Conclusion

The Random Forest and SHAP-based framework successfully identified high-risk landslide zones across Gilgit District, achieving an overall accuracy of 84.4% and a ROC-AUC of 0.94. Slope, vegetation cover, and proximity to roads and rivers emerged as the strongest drivers of susceptibility in the region.

The resulting susceptibility map (see the Results section above) offers a practical tool for disaster risk management, infrastructure planning and sustainable land-use decisions in Gilgit District and similar mountainous regions of northern Pakistan.
